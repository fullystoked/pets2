The European Burmese is an elegant, unique cat breed of far eastern origin. They are an intelligent, loyal, and very affectionate cat that will want to be with you, participating in everything you do - the Burmese truly thrives on human companionship. While they will sometimes take a shine to a particular family member, they will usually warm to, and interact with everyone, including guests. They also enjoy the company of other cats and dogs.

Burmese are playful, lively and inquisitive, even as adults. They have a number of dog-like characteristics, and can be taught to play fetch and tag. They also love to snuggle, and will want to be on your lap as soon as you sit down and look settled. They will follow you to bed to get cosy there as well.

Burmese Cat Temperament

Breed pros

Gives unconditional love

Independence

Not very independent

Training, tricks & games

Can be taught to fetch

The European Burmese is people-oriented cat that forms strong bonds with the humans who love them. They thrive on human attention, and want lots of it. They are full of high spirits and friskiness, they love to entertain and catch your attention, executing daring leaps and pausing to make sure their audience is watching the performance. If their antics aren't being admired, they will find a warm lap and continue to demand undivided attention. Intelligent and inquisitive, they will follow you around the house, wanting to be part of all aspects of your daily life. The upshot is, if you want a cat that isn’t the centre of attention, don’t get a European Burmese.

Because of this strong craving for attention, it's important that your European Burmese isn't left alone for extended periods - they can become unhappy, bored, even depressed or destructive if left alone all day. You must be sure you can provide enough companionship for a European Burmese before deciding to bring one into your family. Another cat, or a friendly, companionable dog can be a great mate for your Burmese.

Burmese Cat History

The European Burmese shares much of its history with the Burmese, with both coming from the same foundation stock. Dr. Joseph Thompson, a U.S. Navy doctor brought a female cat named Wong Mau to the United States from Rangoon, Burma (now Yangon, Myanmar). Some sources say Wong Mau was given to Dr. Thompson by wild animal collector Buck Wilson. Others believe he found Wong Mau himself when he was serving as a ship's doctor in Southeast Asia.

Thompson bred Wong Mau to a sealpoint Siamese and after several generations developed a dark chocolate brown variation that was particularly striking and beautiful. He crossed these sable cats with each other or back to Wong Mau.

The histories of the Burmese and the European Burmese diverge in 1949, when a pair of Wong Mau’s descendants was imported to England by Siamese breeder Lilian France. The breed quickly gained popularity, and other fanciers began breeding programs. The European Burmese breeding program began by accident in 1964 when a Burmese female in heat escaped and mated with a shorthaired red tabby. Additional colours were added accidentally and intentionally and many breeders contributed to developing additional colours for the breed.

The European Burmese come in ten colours outside the four original hues of the Burmese, it is also an elegant cat of moderate type with a slightly rounded head. The Burmese, by contrast, is stocky and compact with a rounded head, and foreshortened face.

Despite the long European history, the European Burmese is a fairly recent addition to the North American cat fancy. The associations that don’t accept the European type accept the contemporary Burmese, although today most associations accept both types.
